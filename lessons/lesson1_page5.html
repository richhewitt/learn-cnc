Encoders provide feedback about the location of the machine axis, which can then be used by the computer to determine the position, velocity, and acceleration of the axis. There are two general types of encoders: Incremental Enconders and Absolute Encoders.(6)

 

Incremental Encoders

Incremental Encoders use a system of alternating binary signals. An optical reader in the Encoder will count these bands of “1” and “0” and then calculate the relative position of the machine.  A zero position can be established at start-up with a process called “Homing”.(6)

 

Absolute Encoders

Absolute Encoders differ from Incremental Encoders in that each position is assigned a unique value (see figure above). This value is then sent to the control system where it can make adjustments if needed. (6)

 

Rotary Encoders

Rotary Encoder animation

Rotary Encoders are common in CNC technology and are attached to the machine motors.  An optical head emits a light beam through a rotary disc containing slits. As the motor spins, the beam shining through will appear to blink rapidly and is detected by a photo sensor on the opposite side of the disc.  The Encoder will then count the number of blinking lights per unit time to calculate the machine’s position, velocity, and acceleration.
